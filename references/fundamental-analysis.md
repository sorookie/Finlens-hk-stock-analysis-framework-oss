# Fundamental Analysis Reference

## Objective

Evaluate whether the business is high quality, whether the financials support the story, whether the valuation is justified, whether dividends and buybacks are real support, and what risks are most likely to matter.

## Analysis Framework

### 1. Understand the Business First

Before touching valuation, answer these questions:

* What does the company actually sell
* Which products, segments, or customers matter most
* How does the company make money
* What drives revenue growth and margin expansion
* Which parts of the model are cyclical, structural, policy-sensitive, or one off
* Is the listed vehicle the main operating asset or only one layer of a larger group structure

### 2. Business Quality Assessment

**Competitive Advantages**

Assess whether the company has durable advantages such as:

* brand strength
* network effects
* switching costs
* scale or cost advantage
* proprietary assets, channels, IP, or ecosystem lock in
* regulatory, distribution, or asset barriers

Questions to answer:

* Is the moat visible in gross margin, market share, pricing power, or repeat purchase behavior
* Is the moat strengthening, stable, or weakening
* Is the company taking share or defending share

**Management Quality**

Review:

* capital allocation record
* dividend discipline
* buyback discipline versus symbolic buybacks
* M&A quality
* guidance quality and credibility
* compensation alignment
* transparency in annual reports, interim reports, calls, and filings

**Business Model Quality**

Review:

* revenue concentration by product, geography, or customer
* recurring versus transactional revenue
* margin sustainability
* scalability
* dependence on one product cycle, one policy regime, or one external platform
* exposure to Mainland regulation, commodity cycles, consumer sentiment, or rates when relevant

### 3. Financial Health Analysis

**Profitability**

Track trend and level for:

* gross margin
* operating margin
* net margin
* ROE
* ROA
* ROIC

Look for:

* improving mix or operating leverage
* margin compression from competition, pricing, cost inflation, or policy pressure
* unusually large adjustments that flatter profitability

**Growth Quality**

Evaluate:

* revenue growth YoY and over multiple years
* profit growth versus revenue growth
* segment growth breakdown
* geographic contribution
* whether growth is volume driven, price driven, acquisition driven, or accounting driven

A high quality growth profile usually means:

* revenue growth is durable
* margin is stable or improving
* cash flow keeps up with earnings
* dilution is controlled

**Balance Sheet Strength**

Review:

* cash versus total debt
* current ratio and quick ratio when relevant
* interest coverage
* debt maturity profile if material
* working capital trend
* lease or off balance sheet obligations if important
* whether net cash or low leverage supports dividend and buyback durability

**Cash Flow Quality**

Review:

* operating cash flow consistency
* free cash flow generation
* free cash flow margin
* cash conversion versus net income
* capex intensity
* working capital swings
* minority interest or restructuring noise when relevant

### 4. Shareholder Return Quality

Do not treat headline dividend yield as enough.
Review:

* dividend history
* payout ratio
* special dividend dependence
* buyback history and pace
* whether buybacks are large enough to matter on a per share basis
* whether capital return is funded by real free cash flow or by balance sheet stretch

High quality capital return usually means:

* cash generation is real
* payout is sustainable
* buybacks are consistent and meaningful
* balance sheet remains healthy after capital return

### 5. Valuation Analysis

Use both relative and practical valuation framing when possible.

**Relative Valuation**

Compare versus:

* the company’s own history
* direct peers
* sector norms when relevant

Useful multiples:

* trailing P/E
* forward P/E
* PEG
* P/B for banks, insurers, exchanges, and some asset sensitive businesses
* EV/EBITDA
* EV/Sales
* dividend yield
* FCF yield
* price to FCF when cash return matters more than accounting profit

**Practical Valuation Questions**

* Is the stock cheap because the market is wrong, or because earnings quality is weak
* Is a high dividend yield a true support or a value trap signal
* Is the market already pricing in peak cycle earnings
* Is southbound demand helping sustain the current valuation
* Is the company buying back enough stock to affect the multiple debate

Use a fair value range rather than one false precision point.

### 6. Industry and Competitive Context

Do enough industry work to avoid analyzing the company in isolation.

Review:

* industry growth rate
* industry structure
* degree of competition
* barriers to entry
* regulatory and policy exposure
* cyclicality and sensitivity to Mainland demand or global commodity moves when relevant

### 7. Market Structure Context

When relevant, add:

* southbound eligibility and southbound holding trend
* short selling or short position context
* liquidity and turnover quality
* board lot barrier for retail participation
* whether the stock is crowded as a yield trade, policy trade, or consensus defensive trade

When market structure matters, load `references/ownership-positioning.md` and separate clearly between:

* ownership and custody concentration
* short-selling flow
* weekly net short inventory
* buyback absorption

## Workflow

1. Understand the business and revenue model
2. Check moat and competitive position
3. Review 3 to 5 years of financial trend data when possible
4. Evaluate profitability, growth, balance sheet, and cash flow quality
5. Evaluate dividend and buyback quality when relevant
6. Compare against peers
7. Frame valuation versus what is already priced in
8. Add ownership and positioning context when relevant
9. Identify the top risks and red flags
10. Build both the bull case and bear case
11. Conclude with what must happen for the current valuation to work

## Red Flags to Watch

* declining gross or operating margins without a clear strategic reason
* earnings growing much faster than cash flow
* persistent negative free cash flow without a credible payoff path
* rising debt without clear return on capital
* dividend yield held up by falling price rather than business strength
* symbolic buybacks that are too small to matter
* repeated large non recurring adjustments
* receivables or inventory rising faster than revenue
* excessive policy dependence or unresolved regulatory risk
* customer concentration above a material threshold
* poor acquisition discipline
* management guidance credibility problems

## Output Expectations

A strong fundamental conclusion should answer six things:

1. Is the business good
2. Are the financials supporting the story
3. Is the balance sheet safe enough
4. Are dividends and buybacks real support or just surface level comfort
5. Is the valuation already pricing in the good news
6. What is the biggest reason the thesis could fail
